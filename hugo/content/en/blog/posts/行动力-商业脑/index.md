Many people fail in business not because they lack execution, but because they have execution without business sense. The stronger the execution, the faster they lose money when heading in the wrong direction.

The portrait of this type of person isexceptionally clear. They act immediately, with extraordinary execution. On a whim, they rent a shop, renovate it, stock it, and open for business, at a speed that is genuinely impressive. But they have no idea how to count. How to sell, how to manage people, how to control costs, how to calculate profit. They do not know and do not care. The result is that they only know how to pay, not how to collect. They only know how to work, not how to make money.

Execution is only one dimension of entrepreneurship. Another equally important dimension is the ability to understand business logic: knowing where money comes from, how it comes, and how long it will keep coming. Without this understanding, the stronger your execution, the faster you burn through your capital.

The core of business sense is not intelligence. It is building a few basic analytical habits. The first is cost awareness: before making any investment, calculate how much will come back and when. The second is differentiation awareness: what makes your product different from others, and does that difference matter to customers. The third is leverage awareness: which investments can generatesustained, compounding returns, and which are pureconsumption.

These habits are not innate. They can be developed. The simplest method is to write down the answers to three questions before making any decision: what is my cost, where is my differentiation, and can this investment generate compound returns.

After writing this thirty times, business sense becomes second nature.
