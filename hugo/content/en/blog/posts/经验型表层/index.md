In the workplace, capability management can be strategically divided into two layers: surface-level experience and core capabilities. Use the surface layer to build trust, and protect the core layer as your competitive moat.

The first step is capabilitylayering. Separate what you can share from what you must never share. Surface-level experience includes general skills, industrycommon knowledge, publicly shareable methodologies, and commonwork experience. These can be freely shared. They help you build a professional image and earn the trust of colleagues and managers.

Core capabilities include your original frameworks, yoursynthesis of key patterns, the key relationships you have built, and the proprietary methods that have proven effective in specific scenarios. These are yourcore assets. They can be directlycarried over to your next role.

The second step is to use surface-level experience to build trust. Share valuableexperience and insights at appropriate times. Be willing to help colleagues solve problems. Actively participate in internal training and discussions. This not only earns you a good reputation, but also gives you access to morecritical information.

The third step is to use core capabilities to build your moat. Keep your core knowledge in your personal note-taking system. Do not share it with the company. Let people see that you have capability, but never let them see the full source of your capability.

The key to staying compliant throughout this process is that you are sharing your own experience and general skills, not any company secrets. What you are protecting is not the company property. It is your own intellectual property.

The company is buying your labour time and your general skills. It is not buying your personal network or your proprietary methodology. You have no obligation to hand over the latter two.
