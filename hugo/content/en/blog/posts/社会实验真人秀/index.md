Locking real people in an enclosed space, throwing them a crisis scenario, and letting them solve problems using their genuine personalities, knowledge, and experience. This format has existed in television and experimental theatre for years, with severalmature models.

The most precise classification is social experiment reality show plus immersive group survival game. It combines three elements: real social identities, an enclosed space, and a crisis challenge. The participants real identities as academics, scientists, businesspeople, artists, etc. determine their behaviour under crisis, which is completely different from scripted performances.

There aremature examples both domestically and internationally. The classicinternational formats are Survivor and Big Brother, which, although they add competitive and voting mechanisms, share the core premise of putting people in an enclosed environment and observing theirgenuine reactions. Domestically, there are various workplace and entrepreneurship reality shows. Despite significant post-production scripting, the overall framework is similar.

The core appeal of this format is that real human nature under pressure reveals unpredictable richness. The details that no script can write, a person instinctive response in a critical moment, thesubtle shift in attitude between two people, the self-organising process of a group, these are the most compelling content.

To produce a viable version of this format, there are two key design elements. First, set up an initial crisis that has enough tension to make all participants take it seriously. Second, ensure the participants have sufficient diversity in background so that different knowledge structures and experiences can genuinely collide.
