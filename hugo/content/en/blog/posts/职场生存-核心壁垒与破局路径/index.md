The transition from workplace survival to independent entrepreneurship is essentially an upgrade in cognition. What most people accumulate at work is the ability to do a job well, not the ability to run a business. These two require completely different skill structures.

The workplace survival phase is about building professional competence and reputation. You need to become more professional than most in one or two areas, so that the company feels it is worth keeping you. The strategy for this phase can be summarised as: use professional competence to earn job security, use professional reputation to earn room for growth.

But doing your job well is not enough. If you are just efficiently completing tasks, you are only contributing to someone else business model. What truly matters is understanding how the business model works while you are doing the work. How does the company make money? Why do customers pay? What is the cost structure? Where does the competitive advantage come from? These are the core cognitions that an entrepreneur needs, and they are also what most employees never consciously learn.

The key to breaking through is shifting from executor thinking to operator thinking. Executor thinking focuses on doing things right. Operator thinking focuses on whether the thing is worth doing. The former is a pursuit of efficiency. The latter is a judgement of direction. Without direction, the higher the efficiency, the greater the loss.

In practical terms, a steady approach has four steps. First, deliberately observe and learn business logic at work, treating every workinteraction as an opportunity to learn how the business model works. Second, use your spare time to validate your business hypotheses at minimal cost. You do not need to quit your job. Weekends and evenings are enough. Third, when your side income reaches half of your salary, begin seriously assessing the feasibility of full-time entrepreneurship. Fourth, only make thefull transition after you have saved six months of living expenses as a safety net.

Your core moat is not your skills. It is the depth of your understanding of a particular market and your network of resources.
